Invasive Species Project - Zebra Mussels

Zebra mussels (Dreissena polymorpha) are invasive species about the size of a fingernail. They are native to freshwaters in Russia and Ukraine but were introduced to the Great Lakes Region in the 1980s from a transatlantic vessel. In a decade, they colonized the river basins of the Great Lakes and beyond in Mississippi, Tennessee, and Ohio.

These mussels are armed with rootlike threads that allow them to strongly attach themselves to the sides of hard surfaces, such as rocks, docks, and boats. Their microscopic larvae are also able to spread themselves between bodies of water by anything that is moving between two bodies of water, such as waterfowl, bait buckets, diving gear, and many other things.

Solutions

Unfortunately, Zebra Mussels are not edible to humans due to the accumulation of toxins, pollutants, and microorganism within the mussel's body as they filter water. The main form of eradication of this invasive species is to use a type of molluscide and the main form of prevention is to "clean, drain, and dry" boats before moving to another body of water.
